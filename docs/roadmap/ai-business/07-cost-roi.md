## 🎬 Video (~6–8 min) — NotebookLM → Video → Customize
```
Audience: a smart business owner with zero AI background and no patience for hype — explain like a
trusted advisor, plain English before any term of art. Cover ONLY: the money conversation nobody
has honestly. Three plain ideas: (1) what AI actually costs — subscriptions and usage; usage means
you pay per work done (long documents and big jobs cost more than short questions — that's the
whole mental model), so a monthly automation has a price tag you can SEE, and a budget cap
(Module 05) keeps it a number you chose; (2) what it earns — count ONLY hours genuinely freed on
tasks you actually do (the Module 04 playbook that turned 40 weekly minutes into 5 is real; "the
team feels more productive" is not a number), value the hours at what the person costs, and be
honest that freed time only pays when it's re-spent on something that matters; (3) when AI LOSES
money — the anti-hype segment, given straight: rare tasks (setup never pays back), tasks where
checking the output takes as long as doing it, mistake-expensive tasks forcing double-checks of
everything, and tool-hopping every month. Worked example: the agency runs the numbers on its
proposal-drafting playbook — minutes saved per proposal × proposals per month × loaded hourly
rate vs subscription + usage + the owner's review time — and it clears; then the same maths on
their once-a-quarter award submission — and it doesn't. ~7 minutes: the 2–3 mistakes (counting
gross time saved and ignoring review time; ROI-ing the tool instead of the task; quitting in week
two before the habit forms), close with: "Cost per task versus the minutes it frees, on tasks you
do weekly. If a task clears, scale it; if it doesn't, drop it without guilt." Ground strictly in
the sources; no invented industry statistics.
```

## 🎧 Deep Dive (learn) — NotebookLM → Audio → Customize
```
Two hosts, advisor tone, plain English before any term of art. Teach a business owner to run the
AI value calculation like any other purchase decision: the cost side (subscription + usage +
setup time + the ongoing human review time — the honest line most calculations skip); the value
side (minutes freed × frequency × loaded cost of the person, counted only on the Module 01 map's
weekly-plus tasks, plus the harder-to-price wins named honestly as judgment calls: faster replies,
fewer dropped follow-ups, evenings back); reading the platform's cost tab as a manager (what ran,
what each automation cost this month, cost per run — five minutes monthly, same as reading a
utility bill); the portfolio view (a few automations that clearly pay > a dozen half-used
experiments; kill-list discipline quarterly); and the don't-automate list as a strategic asset —
what stays human because mistakes are expensive, volume is trivial, or the human touch IS the
product. End with the artifact: a one-page cost/ROI sheet per running automation — cost per month,
hours freed, verdict: scale, keep, or kill. Ground strictly in the sources.
```

## 🎧 Debate (the judgment call) — NotebookLM → Audio → Customize
```
Two expert hosts argue, advisor tone, for a business-owner listener: "Where does SMB AI ROI
actually live — the tasks or the habit?" One host argues TASK-BY-TASK: value only counts on a
spreadsheet line — minutes freed on named weekly tasks, net of review time; everything else is
vibes, and vibes are how owners get sold junk. The other argues the CAPABILITY VIEW: task-ROI
systematically undercounts — the café owner who reclaims Friday afternoons starts doing supplier
negotiation she never had time for; the compounding asset is a team fluent in delegating to AI,
and that never fits one line. Make them collide on a real case: the consultancy's automation
portfolio after six months — which line items justified the spend, and what changed that no line
item shows. Land honestly: agree on measuring weekly tasks and killing losers quarterly; disagree
on how much weight the unmeasurable deserves. Ground in the sources; no invented statistics.
```

## 🛠 Do it now (on the free platform)
Open the cost tab and put real numbers on your Module 04 automation: what it cost this month, what
it costs per run, and the minutes it frees each week (net of your review time — be honest). Fill
the one-page ROI sheet: cost / hours freed / verdict (scale, keep, kill). If it clears, pick the
next candidate from your Module 01 map and repeat the Module 04 build. If it doesn't, kill it
without guilt and pick a better task — that discipline is worth more than any single automation.
