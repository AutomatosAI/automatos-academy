## 🎬 Video (~6–8 min) — NotebookLM → Video → Customize
```
Audience: a smart business owner with zero AI background and no patience for hype — explain like a
trusted advisor, plain English before any term of art. Cover ONLY: how to let AI act on your
behalf without losing sleep — the same way you'd delegate to a new employee: small scope first,
checkpoints, a spending limit, and trust earned by track record. The four guardrails, each in one
plain sentence: SCOPE (it can only touch what you allow — the supplies agent reads supplier emails
and drafts orders; it cannot see payroll); APPROVAL (for anything that leaves the building — an
email to a customer, an order, a payment — it proposes, you approve, until its record earns
standing approval for the low-stakes lane); BUDGET (a monthly cap on what it can spend — when it's
hit, everything pauses and asks); and VISIBILITY (every action is logged; you can always see what
it did and why). Worked example: the consultancy hands its agent the goal "chase the five overdue
invoices" — watch the goal become a PLAN (draft five reminder emails, escalate the 60-day one),
the plan wait for APPROVAL, the owner strike one client off, approve the rest, and the mission run
and report. ~7 minutes: the 2–3 mistakes (all-or-nothing thinking — either terrified or fully
hands-off; approving without reading for week one; no budget cap "because it's a trial"), close
with: "Delegate like you would to a new hire: small lane, checkpoints, a card with a limit. Widen
the lane as it earns it." Ground strictly in the sources; do not invent platform features.
```

## 🎧 Deep Dive (learn) — NotebookLM → Audio → Customize
```
Two hosts, advisor tone, plain English before any term of art. Teach a business owner the
delegation framework for AI that acts: the mission pattern (you give a GOAL, it proposes a PLAN,
you approve or edit, it runs and reports — decisions stay yours, execution moves); designing the
approval line by mistake-cost (auto-fine: internal drafts and summaries · propose-first: anything
customer-facing · never-alone: money out, commitments, anything legal); budgets as a first-class
control (a cap converts "what could it cost me?" into a number you chose); the graduated-trust
ladder (week one approve everything, then standing approval for the proven low-stakes lane, review
weekly — same as any new hire); and reading the activity log as a manager (what ran, what it
touched, what it cost — five minutes a week). Be honest about failure: an approved plan can still
produce a bad email; checkpoints shrink blast radius, they don't abolish mistakes — which is why
scope and budget exist. End with the artifact: one mission run end-to-end with an approval and a
budget cap. Ground strictly in the sources.
```

## 🎧 Debate (the judgment call) — NotebookLM → Audio → Customize
```
Two expert hosts argue, advisor tone, for a business-owner listener: "How much autonomy should a
small business give an AI agent?" One host argues MINIMAL, indefinitely — SMBs have no safety team,
one bad customer email costs a relationship, and approval-on-everything costs minutes a day, so
the asymmetry says keep the human in every loop. The other argues EARNED AND WIDENING — the value
compounds only when routine work truly leaves your desk; approval-fatigue makes owners rubber-stamp
(worse than honest autonomy in a small lane); give narrow standing approval with budgets and logs,
like every delegation since the first apprentice. Make them engage on one concrete case: the
e-commerce shop's "where's my order?" replies — propose-first forever, or auto-send after two
clean weeks? Land honestly: they agree on budgets, logs, and never-alone-for-money; they disagree
on where the approval line settles. Ground in the sources.
```

## 🛠 Do it now (on the free platform)
Run your first mission. Give Automatos a real, bounded goal (chase overdue invoices, prep the
weekly social drafts, summarise this week's reviews and propose replies) — read the plan it
proposes, edit it, approve it, and set a budget cap before it runs. Read the report and the
activity log afterwards. Then decide, deliberately, one lane you'd give standing approval — and
one thing that stays never-alone. Write both on the policy sheet you'll finish in Module 06.
