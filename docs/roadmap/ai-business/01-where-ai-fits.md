## 🎬 Video (~6–8 min) — NotebookLM → Video → Customize
```
Audience: a smart business owner with zero AI background and no patience for hype — explain like a
trusted advisor, plain English before any term of art. Cover ONLY: how to find where AI fits YOUR
business, using one simple test — "repetitive knowledge work": anything done more than weekly, that
follows a rough pattern, where the raw material is words or numbers (emails, quotes, reports,
listings, reviews, invoices, meeting notes). Walk the four map areas with ONE example each from the
recurring cast: getting customers (the agency drafting proposals and social posts), serving
customers (the e-commerce shop answering "where's my order" and review replies), running the place
(the café turning supplier emails into a weekly order sheet), and the numbers (the consultancy
summarising timesheets into a Friday status note). Then the honest anti-hype segment: what NOT to
start with — anything where a wrong answer is expensive or legal (contracts, pricing decisions,
hiring calls), anything you do twice a year, and anything you'd be embarrassed to have a customer
discover was automated. ~7 minutes: open with "stop asking 'should we use AI' — ask 'which of our
repeated paperwork should go first'," then the four areas, then the 2–3 mistakes (starting with the
hardest process; automating a broken process — fix it first; boiling the ocean instead of picking
ONE), close with: "One repeated task, done weekly, made of words. Start there. This week." Ground
strictly in the sources; no invented ROI numbers.
```

## 🎧 Deep Dive (learn) — NotebookLM → Audio → Customize
```
Two hosts, advisor tone, plain English before any term of art. Teach a business owner to build
their own AI opportunity map: the repetitive-knowledge-work test (frequency × pattern × words-in,
words-out); walking each business function — sales/marketing, customer service, operations/admin,
finance/reporting — and listing candidate tasks; scoring candidates on three axes a non-technical
owner can judge (how often, how painful, how bad is a mistake); the crucial distinction between
quick wins (this month: drafting, summarising, first-pass answers — a person still checks) and
transformation (this year: whole workflows running with checkpoints — that's Modules 04–05); and
why the mistake-cost axis decides HOW you deploy (draft-for-human vs act-with-approval vs fully
automatic), not WHETHER. Include the fix-the-process-first rule: automating chaos gives you faster
chaos. End with the artifact: a filled opportunity map with three candidates circled and one
chosen. Ground strictly in the sources.
```

## 🎧 Debate (the judgment call) — NotebookLM → Audio → Customize
```
Two expert hosts argue, advisor tone, for a business-owner listener: "AI for small business —
genuine shift, or overhyped quick wins?" One host argues most SMB value is real but modest —
drafting and summarising, a few saved hours a week, and the transformation stories are enterprise
marketing that doesn't survive contact with a 12-person company. The other argues the compounding
view — each automated task frees attention, the tasks chain into systems (order → reply → report),
and the SMBs that build the habit now will run leaner than competitors within two years. Make them
engage on a concrete case: the e-commerce shop's customer-service inbox, quick-win version vs
system version. Land honestly: both agree on starting small THIS week; they disagree on where it
compounds. Ground in the sources; no invented statistics.
```

## 🛠 Do it now (on the free platform)
Build your opportunity map — with the AI's help. In Automatos chat, describe your business in five
sentences, then ask it to interview you function-by-function (customers, service, operations,
numbers) and produce a table of repetitive-knowledge-work candidates scored by frequency, pain, and
mistake-cost. Argue with it — cut what it got wrong. Save the result; it's your Module 08 capstone
input, and the task you circle becomes the workflow you automate in Module 04.
